Useful Links Page

Here are some links that were helpful to me in authoring this website and curating this collection.

Online Cube Stores

My opportunities to seek out different cube feels are severely constrained since I live in a far-off island, with no opportunities to go to cubing meet-ups and try other people's cubes. It's probably why my collection is as extensive as it is; I had to buy all the cubes I wanted to feel. Fortunately both the sites below have been able to ship to my location!

The Cubicle
https://www.thecubicle.com
These are the guys I ordered from first. They have a large array of custom cube setups.
SpeedCubeShop
https://www.speedcubeshop.com
These guys came in clutch when I was looking for the missing members of my YJ MGC collection.

Both these companies have great selection, timely shipping (or as much as can be expected for someone living in the Pacific Ocean), and good customer service. At this point your choice of store may be determined by which YouCubers you regularly follow.

Information Sources

I spent a lot of time researching these sites when I was gathering information about the cubes that was not already available from the cubes, cube boxes, or the photos I took. I hope you find them useful, too, if you are trying to track down facts about some long-forgotten cube.

Speedsolving.com Wiki
https://www.speedsolving.com/wiki

A one-stop resource for cubing methods and algorithms, articles about aspects of speedcubing such as lubrication, and descriptions of individual cubes and histories of cubing companies. Some useful pages:

  • An overall history of cubing: https://www.speedsolving.com/wiki/index.php?title=History_of_cubing
  • Also, try to enter the name of a cube manufacturer into the search bar (such as "MoYu", "QiYi", etc.). Most of the major manufacturers have a page dedicated to short descriptions of their cubes. As an example, this is the MoYu page.

Resources: cube history, methods and algs, general info.
TheCubicle.com Store Pages
https://www.thecubicle.com/products/moyu-rs3-m-2020 (for example)

The product pages on the Cubicle have meticulously-collected information about the product, as product pages should, but an especially useful piece of data has been the "Added" field in the cube specifications. I was able to find out the release year of most of my cube as follows:

  • If the date given is after September 11, 2018 (2018-09-11), then this date is close to the actual release date of the cube. For example, if you click thet link above to see the original RS3 M 2020, you will see from the "Added" field that the cube must have been released close to June 2020.
  • 2018-09-11 must have been the date of some website database upgrade or migration, as there are no dates earlier than that. If I see this date for "Added", I had to consult another website for my "release year" information.

Resources: cube details, cube release history
/r/Cubers Wiki - Timeline of Puzzle Releases
https://www.reddit.com/r/Cubers/wiki/new_cubes/

I stumbled upon this resource when I discovered that no "Added" date in the Cubicle store pages were older than 2018-09-11. Although this page has not been updated in a long time, it coincidentally ceased to be updated in May 2018. It only left 4 months in the cube release schedule that was unaccounted for. Better luck in research I have never had, even during my university days!

I downloaded a copy to my computer for safekeeping in case the online version goes down, and will make it available on that eventuality.

Resources: cube release history
SpeedCubeReview.com
https://www.speedcubereview.com/

This contains a widely diverse collection of cube reviews, both his own (on video) and written ones (submitted by users of the webiste). Although they lean towards pro-level speedcubes, he reviews cubes of all budget tiers. It also contains a curated set of algorithms for CFOP (I am working on his version of 4-look last layer).

Resources: written cube reviews, methods and algs
JPerm.net
https://www.JPerm.net/

In addition to the lists of speedsolving algorithms (F2L, OLL, and PLL) collected in one place, JPerm has also built a trainer and checklist (where you can use the website as your cube timer to repeat the algorithms over and over, as well as keep track of your progress through the set of algorithms).

Resources: methods and algs, alg trainer

YouCubers

Prior to buying my first cube, I had no idea that there were specific YouTube channels that specialized in cubing content (e.g., cube reviews, tutorials, how to solve faster, experiments, etc.). Here are a few that I've followed in the past few years.

SpeedCubeReview
https://www.youtube.com/@SpeedCubeReview
Easily the grand-daddy of them all who are still posting videos. Has reviewed probably every cube you would probably be curious about. The longevity of his channel was an enormous help to me when researching what cubes from the deep past (before 2018) might be interesting to get for my Historical Cubes series.
Status: active
Resources: cube reviews, beginner tutorials
CubeHead
https://www.youtube.com/@CubeHead
A graphic designer turned cuber who makes funny review videos of cubes. He is the designer of the MoYu/Cubicle Ferrocore cube, and has as his latest project a Cube Academy which aims to make all speedcubing information in the form of PDF sheets and videos available in one place.
Status: active
Resources: cube reviews, funny cubing content
JPerm
https://www.youtube.com/@JPerm
JPerm is the first YouCuber someone gets recommended once they get into the hobby. His channel contains concise instructions on how to accomplish the many algorithms, as well as clear and well-thought out videos explaining technical aspects of cubes. His explanation of the Weilong GTS3 dual adjustment system was the video that made compression, axis distance, and tension finally click for me. He also described how the Aolong v2 responded to Feliks' infamous DNF by changing the piece shape. In this manner he is a cuber's cuber (or perhaps, was... he has not uploaded in some time).
Status: inactive
Resources: cube reviews, speed tutorials, explainers
JRCuber
https://www.youtube.com/@JRCuber
JRCuber is a great YouCuber in his own right—articulate, concise, and knowledgeable about cubes and cubing technology, but his channel is quite valuable because of its longevity. He has been posting cube unboxing videos since he was a child, more than a decade ago, and his youthful voice still speaks about all these cubes that would be considered vintage now. He now also works at the Cubicle, which I can definitely imagine is a cuber boy's dream come true.
Status: inactive
Resources: cube reviews, cube collection tours

There are quite a few more YouCubers who should make this list in the following days; please look forward to the updates.
